## Overview of the Proposed Economic System

The economic system described in the research prompt represents a radical departure from the dominant capitalist paradigm. It is characterized by the **abolition of the bourgeois state** and the establishment of a **workers’ federation** grounded in free association. The federation retains administrative and defensive functions, with a four-branch government: an elected executive council, a bicameral legislature (with an upper house composed of descendants of Indigenous North Americans and enslaved peoples), a judiciary, and a utility commission managing all public services. The economy is structured around **workers’ cooperatives** and **mutual aid societies**, with no passive income, no private landownership, and land access based on occupancy and use. The financial system is managed by non-monopolistic People's Banks issuing **demurrage credits** that expire after five years. Credits are only valid for services, not goods, and are issued based on labor performed. The distributive principle is 'from each according to ability, to each according to labor.' Mutual aid societies are subsidized by the financial system and serve as the primary social safety net. Foreign investment is allowed through contracts with cooperatives, and credits can be exchanged internationally via savings accounts.

This system draws on historical and theoretical precedents from **mutualism**, **cooperative socialism**, and the **Freiwirtschaft** tradition, while integrating contemporary innovations in governance, finance, and social welfare. The following report analyzes the likely material conditions resulting from this system, including standards of living, economic stability, innovation, inequality, labor dynamics, housing, and international trade. It also situates the system within the broader context of economic theory and historical analogues.

## Governance Structure and Political Economy

### Four-Branch Government: Democratic and Inclusive Design

The governance structure of the workers’ federation is notable for its **four-branch model**:

- **Executive Council**: Elected by the federation’s members, responsible for day-to-day administration and coordination of policy.
- **Bicameral Legislature**: The lower house is elected by the general population, while the upper house is reserved for descendants of Indigenous North Americans and enslaved peoples. This design explicitly addresses historical injustices and ensures representation for groups whose land and labor were foundational to the region’s wealth.
- **Judiciary**: An independent system for adjudicating disputes, upholding rights, and interpreting laws.
- **Utility Commission**: Manages all public services, including energy, water, transportation, and communications, ensuring universal access and democratic oversight.

This structure reflects a **commitment to participatory democracy**, restorative justice, and the decentralization of power. The bicameral legislature, in particular, is reminiscent of historical models where different social classes or groups were represented in separate chambers, such as the British Parliament’s House of Lords and House of Commons, but here reimagined to prioritize historically marginalized communities.

### Political Economy: Abolition of the Bourgeois State

The abolition of the bourgeois state signifies the end of a political structure designed to reproduce capitalist production relations. Instead, the workers’ federation is tasked with reproducing cooperative and mutualist relations, with the state functioning as a facilitator of free association, collective decision-making, and the equitable distribution of resources. The separation of administrative and defensive functions from economic control is intended to prevent the concentration of power and the emergence of new elites.

The inclusion of a utility commission as a distinct branch reflects the recognition that control over infrastructure and public services is central to material well-being and social equity. By placing these services under democratic management, the system aims to avoid the pitfalls of privatization and bureaucratic inefficiency.

## Economic Institutions: Workers’ Cooperatives and Mutual Aid Societies

### Workers’ Cooperatives: Democratic Ownership and Control

At the heart of the proposed system are **workers’ cooperatives**, defined by the International Labour Organization as “autonomous associations of persons united voluntarily to meet their common economic, social, and cultural needs and aspirations through a jointly owned and democratically controlled enterprise”. In this model, workers own and control the means of production, make decisions collectively, and share in the surplus generated by their labor.

Empirical research on cooperatives, including the Mondragon Corporation in Spain, demonstrates that cooperatives can be as productive as conventional firms, often exhibit greater employment stability, and tend to be more resilient during economic crises. The participatory nature of cooperatives fosters a sense of ownership, responsibility, and solidarity among workers, which can translate into higher job satisfaction and lower rates of firm failure.

Cooperatives also tend to have more egalitarian wage structures, with maximum salary differentials typically ranging from 1:6 to 1:9, compared to much higher disparities in capitalist firms. Decision-making is democratic, with each member having an equal vote, and profits are distributed according to labor contribution rather than capital investment.

### Mutual Aid Societies: Social Safety Net and Community Resilience

**Mutual aid societies** are voluntary associations that pool resources to support members in times of need, providing financial assistance, healthcare, housing, education, and other services. These societies have a long history, particularly among marginalized communities, and have often filled gaps left by government and market institutions.

In the proposed system, mutual aid societies are subsidized by the financial system and serve as the primary social safety net. This approach emphasizes **solidarity, empowerment, and inclusion**, allowing communities to address their own needs and build resilience against economic and social shocks. Mutual aid societies are governed democratically, with transparent operations and regular member oversight, ensuring accountability and responsiveness to local conditions.

The integration of mutual aid societies into the broader economic system creates an **ecosystem of support**, connecting cooperatives, credit unions, nonprofits, and unions into networks that can mobilize resources, share knowledge, and coordinate action.

## Land Tenure and Access Based on Occupancy and Use

### Abolition of Private Landownership: Occupancy and Use Principle

One of the most transformative aspects of the proposed system is the **abolition of private landownership**. Land access is determined by occupancy and use, rather than by title or inheritance. This principle draws on **customary land tenure systems**, where land is held collectively by families, clans, or communities, and allocated based on need and productive capacity.

Under this model, individuals or groups have the right to use land for housing, agriculture, or other purposes as long as they actively occupy and utilize it. Transfer of land requires community consensus, and alienation of land for speculative purposes is prohibited. This approach is intended to prevent land hoarding, speculation, and the concentration of wealth, while ensuring that all members of society have access to the resources necessary for survival and development.

The **administration of land rights** is managed by local councils or committees, composed of elected representatives and elders, who oversee allocation, dispute resolution, and land-use planning. Documentation may be minimal, relying on oral agreements and community recognition, but mechanisms for formal registration and adjudication can be developed as needed to ensure security and prevent conflicts.

### Implications for Material Conditions

The occupancy and use principle is likely to result in **greater equity in land distribution**, reduced homelessness, and more sustainable land management. By tying land rights to active use, the system encourages stewardship, investment in infrastructure, and long-term planning. It also facilitates community engagement in decision-making, fostering a sense of belonging and responsibility.

However, challenges may arise in urban areas with high population density, where competing claims and the need for infrastructure development require careful coordination. Mechanisms for balancing individual and collective interests, resolving disputes, and accommodating changing demographics will be essential for the system’s success.

## Demurrage Currency and People's Banks

### Demurrage Credits: Incentivizing Circulation and Investment

The financial system is based on **demurrage credits**, a form of currency that loses value over time and expires after five years. Credits are issued by non-monopolistic People's Banks, based on labor performed, and are valid only for services, not goods. This design is inspired by the **Freiwirtschaft** tradition and the work of Silvio Gesell, who argued that demurrage currency encourages spending, investment, and economic activity by discouraging hoarding and speculation.

Demurrage currency has been used in various historical contexts, including ancient Egypt, medieval Europe, and during the Great Depression, where it was credited with increasing the velocity of money, reducing unemployment, and stabilizing local economies. By ensuring that money circulates rapidly, demurrage credits can help prevent recessions, eliminate inflation and deflation, and create a more predictable economic environment.

### People's Banks: Decentralized and Democratic Finance

**People's Banks** are decentralized financial institutions that issue credits, manage savings accounts, and facilitate international exchange. Unlike conventional banks, they do not operate as monopolies, and their governance is democratic, with members electing representatives and participating in decision-making. Credits are issued based on verified labor, and interest is eliminated, creating an **interest-free economy**.

The demurrage fee collected on expiring credits can be used to fund public services, subsidize mutual aid societies, and support community development. By tying credit issuance to labor and service provision, the system aligns financial incentives with productive activity and social welfare.

### International Exchange and Foreign Investment

Foreign investment is permitted through contracts with cooperatives, and credits can be exchanged internationally via savings accounts. This approach allows for **integration into the global economy** while maintaining local control over resources and decision-making. International exchange mechanisms are designed to prevent capital flight, speculative investment, and the exploitation of local labor, ensuring that foreign investment serves the interests of workers and communities.

## Labor Dynamics and Distributive Principle

### 'From Each According to Ability, To Each According to Labor'

The distributive principle of the system is **'from each according to ability, to each according to labor.'** This means that individuals contribute to society based on their capacities and receive compensation based on the labor they perform. Unlike the Marxist principle of 'to each according to need,' which presupposes abundance and the elimination of scarcity, the proposed system maintains a link between effort and reward, aiming to balance equity and incentive.

In practice, this principle is implemented through cooperative governance, transparent accounting, and regular evaluation of labor contributions. Surplus generated by cooperatives is distributed among members according to hours worked, skill level, and responsibility, with mechanisms for adjusting compensation to reflect changing circumstances.

### Labor Organization and Dynamics

Labor is organized through **cooperatives, mutual aid societies, and unions**, with workers participating in decision-making, collective bargaining, and the management of enterprises. The absence of passive income and private landownership eliminates rent-seeking behavior and the concentration of wealth, while the focus on active participation fosters engagement, skill development, and innovation.

Unions and mutual aid societies play a central role in protecting workers’ rights, negotiating wages and benefits, and providing support during periods of unemployment, illness, or disability. The integration of these organizations into the broader economic system creates a **network of solidarity and mutual support**, enhancing resilience and reducing vulnerability to economic shocks.
